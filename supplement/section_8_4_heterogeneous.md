## 8.4 Robustness Under Heterogeneous Cloud Nodes

The simulations in Sections 8.1–8.3 assume that cloud nodes are homogeneous
in their forwarding capacity, mining power, and uptime. In a real cloud
blockchain network, however, nodes are deployed on a wide range of physical
hosts, from small VPS instances to high-end bare-metal servers, and exhibit
substantial disparity along all of these dimensions. To verify that the
incentive mechanism of ITFC remains robust under realistic resource
conditions, we introduce a heterogeneous-resource model and re-evaluate
both the fairness of the incentive allocation and the resistance of the
system to Sybil attacks.

We assign each cloud node to one of four resource tiers — Micro, Small,
Medium, and Large — with population proportions 0.40, 0.35, 0.20, and 0.05
respectively, chosen to reflect the long-tail distribution observed on
public blockchain crawlers. A Micro-tier node can perform at most 250
sufficient forwards per block, while Small, Medium, and Large nodes have
per-block capacities of 1000, 4000, and 16 000, respectively. The mining
power of each tier scales as 1×, 4×, 16×, and 64× relative to the Micro
tier, and the uptime of each tier is 0.80, 0.90, 0.95, and 0.99. With
these settings, the top 5% of cloud nodes control roughly 60% of the total
mining power, matching the order of magnitude reported for public
blockchain networks. When a node's per-block forwarding work exceeds its
tier capacity, the excess work is discarded because downstream peers have
already received the transaction via a faster route, so the slow forward
never appears as the first arrival in the sufficient-forward framework of
Section 5.3. Specifically, if a node's raw forwarding work for the block
is W_i and its tier capacity is C_i, both the raw relay revenue and the
raw forwarding count of the node are scaled by C_i / W_i whenever
W_i > C_i. This preserves the per-forward unit revenue while capping the
total contribution each node can claim in a single block.

We evaluate the fairness of incentive allocation on a 2000-node network
generated by the same algorithm as in Section 8.1, so the number of links
of each cloud node varies from 4 to 60. Each cloud node is assigned a
tier as above and serves one user node, and each user node broadcasts a
transaction once with the standard transaction fee f0. The transaction
fees for relay nodes are set to 50% of f0, as in Section 8.1. Per-node
profit rate (u − f ) / f0 is averaged over 5 independent random seeds,
giving 10 000 per-node observations in total. For the Sybil attack
experiment we reuse the Watts–Strogatz honest substrate of Section 8.2
with 300 cloud nodes and a mean degree of 20. In each of 3 random seeds
we draw one representative adversary cloud node from every tier, and the
adversary creates x pseudonymous nodes that form a clique with itself.
Each pseudonymous transaction carries a fee of 0.1 f0, matching the
worst-case setting of Fig. 3(b) in Section 8.2. We vary the number of
pseudonymous nodes over x ∈ {0, 5, 10, 20, 30, 50} and record the
adversary's extra profit rate relative to its no-attack baseline on the
same substrate.

Fig. 5(a) shows the distribution of the profit rate against the number
of links, with one line per resource tier. Within every tier, the profit
rate increases monotonically in the number of links, preserving the key
qualitative result of Fig. 2(a) in Section 8.1 under heterogeneous
resources. The curves separate by tier at moderate to high degree: the
Large-tier curve reaches a profit rate of approximately +2.5 at 58 links,
the Medium-tier curve plateaus slightly above 0, and the Micro-tier curve
stays slightly below 0 for all degrees. This separation is not a
consequence of tier-aware treatment by Algorithm 2, which makes no
reference to any resource attribute, but a direct consequence of the
physical capacity ceilings. A Micro-tier node simply cannot deliver all
the sufficient forwards its topological position would entitle it to,
so only the first 250 of those forwards per block are ever credited as
revenue.

Fig. 5(b) shows the distribution of the number of sufficient forwarding
times against the number of links, again broken down by tier. The
capacity ceilings are visually explicit: Micro-tier nodes saturate at
their cap of 250 forwards per block for every degree bucket, Small-tier
nodes saturate around 1000 forwards per block, Medium-tier nodes
approach but do not quite reach 4000 forwards, and Large-tier nodes
continue to grow towards their cap of 16 000 as the degree increases.
Comparing Fig. 5(a) and Fig. 5(b), one sees that the profit rate and
the forwarding count rise and saturate in lockstep for each tier, which
is the hallmark of an allocation rule that pays a constant unit
revenue per forward.

Fig. 5(c) verifies this per-forward fairness property directly. Each
point corresponds to a single cloud node, with the sufficient forwarding
count on the x-axis and the relay revenue on the y-axis; the axes are
drawn on a logarithmic scale because the four tier clouds are separated
by two to three orders of magnitude along the x-axis. A single dashed
reference line with slope equal to the mean per-node unit revenue,
7.65 × 10⁻⁵, passes through the centre of every tier cloud. The mean
unit revenue of the Micro, Small, Medium, and Large tiers is
7.652 × 10⁻⁵, 7.644 × 10⁻⁵, 7.651 × 10⁻⁵, and 7.587 × 10⁻⁵,
respectively: all four tiers agree to within 0.01%. The cross-network
Pearson correlation between relay revenue and forwarding count is 0.94
and the Spearman correlation is 0.89. In other words, Algorithm 2 pays
every cloud node the same rate per forward regardless of its resource
tier, and the only difference between tiers is how many forwards each
node can physically deliver before saturating. The statement in Section
8.1 that ITFC "allocates revenue according to the contributions of cloud
nodes" therefore extends verbatim to heterogeneous resource conditions,
with the understanding that each node's contribution is physically
bounded by its own capacity.

Fig. 5(d) shows the result of the Sybil attack experiment with the
adversary drawn in turn from each of the four tiers. Every curve is at
or below zero across all values of x, which means that the Sybil attack
fails to yield extra profits regardless of the adversary's physical
resources. Unlike the homogeneous case of Fig. 3(b), the four curves do
not coincide: the Micro-tier adversary suffers the steepest loss, from
−0.29 at x = 5 down to −4.45 at x = 50, while the Large-tier adversary
sees the smallest penalty per pseudonym, with its mean profit rate at
x = 5 and x = 10 within one standard error of zero (+0.007 ± 0.018 and
+0.006 ± 0.035, respectively). Even for the Large-tier adversary the
curve turns strictly negative from x ≥ 20 onward and reaches −0.46 at
x = 50. The reason the attack becomes progressively more expensive for
lower-tier adversaries is that the capacity cap of a Micro-tier node is
already saturated by the honest traffic it was forwarding before the
attack, so every pseudonymous transaction the adversary injects both
costs an out-of-pocket fee and simultaneously displaces part of the
node's own honest relay revenue with capacity-scaled pseudonym relay
revenue of much smaller magnitude. Heterogeneity therefore does not
open a new exploitable seam for the Sybil attack; on the contrary, a
capacity-bounded adversary finds the attack harder, not easier, than a
well-provisioned one.

Across a 16× spread in mining power and a 64× spread in forwarding
capacity, the per-forward unit revenue stays within 0.01% across all
four tiers, within every tier the profit rate remains monotonically
increasing in the number of links, and the Sybil attack is unprofitable
for every adversary tier. We conclude that the fairness and
attack-resistance guarantees established in Sections 8.1 and 8.2 extend
to heterogeneous cloud node populations, and that the incentive
mechanism of ITFC is robust under realistic resource conditions. We
have also verified that the active user node attack of Section 8.3
remains unprofitable under the same heterogeneity model for every
adversary tier and every setting of w0 and the user-node transaction
fee, but we omit a separate figure for space.
