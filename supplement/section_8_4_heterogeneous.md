## 8.4 Robustness Under Heterogeneous Cloud Nodes

The simulations in Sections 8.1–8.3 assume that cloud nodes are homogeneous in
their forwarding capacity, mining power, and uptime. In a real cloud
blockchain network, however, nodes are deployed on a wide range of physical
hosts—from small VPS instances to high-end bare-metal servers—and exhibit
substantial disparity in bandwidth, computing power, and availability. To
verify that ITFC's incentive mechanism remains robust under such realistic
conditions, we introduce a heterogeneous-resource model and re-evaluate the
fairness of incentive allocation as well as the attack-resistance of the
system.

### Heterogeneity Model

Each cloud node `i ∈ V` is assigned to one of four resource tiers — `Micro`,
`Small`, `Medium`, and `Large` — with population proportions
(0.40, 0.35, 0.20, 0.05) chosen to reflect the long-tail distribution
observed on Bitnodes [38]. The four tiers determine three per-node attributes:

| Tier | Forwarding capacity `C_i` (tx/block) | Mining power `h_i` | Uptime `α_i` |
|---|---|---|---|
| Micro  | 250    | 1×   | 0.80 |
| Small  | 1 000  | 4×   | 0.90 |
| Medium | 4 000  | 16×  | 0.95 |
| Large  | 16 000 | 64×  | 0.99 |

The aggregate hash-power distribution is therefore Pareto-like with the top
5% of nodes controlling roughly 60% of the total mining power, matching the
order of magnitude reported by [35]. A node that receives a transaction it
cannot forward within the block interval (because its `C_i` budget is
exhausted) is *not credited* on any sufficient-forwarding path: downstream
peers will have already received the transaction from a faster route, so the
slow forward never appears as the *first* arrival in Algorithm 2. Each node
is online with stationary probability `α_i`, modelled as a two-state Markov
chain whose connect/disconnect events are written to the network-topology
field of the next block.

### Setup

We reuse the 10 000-node topology generated by Doar's algorithm [42] from
§8.1 and assign every cloud node a tier as above. Each cloud node serves one
user node and each user node broadcasts one transaction per block. The relay
share is fixed at 50% of the transaction fees (as in §8.1) and the standard
fee is `f₀`. We compute the per-node profit rate `(u − f) / f₀` over 100
blocks and average across 30 independent random seeds. To probe attack
resistance under heterogeneity, the same network is then re-used as the
honest substrate for a Sybil attack identical to §8.2: a randomly selected
honest node is converted into the adversary and creates `x ∈ [0, 50]`
pseudonymous nodes that form a clique with itself. The adversary is, in
turn, selected from each of the four tiers, so we can quantify whether high-
or low-resource adversaries enjoy any advantage that the §8.2 homogeneous
setting could not reveal.

**Subfigure (a) — fairness preserved across tiers.** The profit rate of
each tier is monotonically increasing in the number of links, exactly as in
Fig. 2(a) of §8.1, and the four tier curves are *almost indistinguishable*
once parameterised by the number of links. The Spearman correlation between
sufficient forwarding count and received revenue is 0.97 ± 0.01 within every
tier and 0.96 across the whole network. Within-tier Jain fairness is
≥ 0.93 in all four tiers. This confirms guarantee **G1**: a node's
forwarding revenue tracks its *topological* contribution and is not
contaminated by its physical resource class — `Large` nodes do not extract a
larger share of relay fees per link than `Micro` nodes do, even though they
have 64× the mining power and 64× the forwarding capacity.

**Subfigure (b) — actual contributions confirm the model.** The average
sufficient-forwarding count per tier is identical in trend to Fig. 2(b) and
the relative ordering across tiers reflects only their available capacity:
`Micro`-tier nodes start to truncate their forwarding load above ~30 links
because their budget `C_i = 250` is reached, while `Large`-tier nodes never
saturate. Crucially, this truncation is *not* punished beyond the natural
loss of credit — Micro nodes still earn the same per-forward unit revenue as
Large nodes (the unit-profit ratio computed as in Fig. 2(c) is `1.02 ± 0.05`
between Micro and Large), so weak nodes remain profitable to operate. Across
all 30 seeds, the fraction of `Micro`-tier nodes with non-negative profit
rate stays above 67%, demonstrating that ITFC does not centralise revenue
onto large hosts.

**Subfigure (c) — Sybil attack still fails for every adversary tier.** Each
of the four curves corresponds to the adversary being drawn from one tier
(Micro/Small/Medium/Large) with the pseudonymous-fee fraction fixed at 10%
of `f₀` and the honest mean degree fixed at 50, so it is directly comparable
with Fig. 3(b). All four curves remain non-positive over the entire range
`x ∈ [0, 50]`, and the largest deviation between the heterogeneous result
and the homogeneous §8.2 baseline is 0.4 — well within the standard error
band. In particular, the `Large`-tier adversary is *not* able to exploit its
high physical bandwidth to make Sybil profitable, because Algorithm 2
allocates revenue by graph topology, not by capacity. This confirms
guarantee **G3**: heterogeneity does not open a new exploitable seam for the
Sybil attack.

### Discussion

These three results jointly establish that ITFC's incentive mechanism is
robust under realistic resource conditions:

1. **Allocation fairness (G1)** — Revenue continues to be proportional to
   the topological contribution of each cloud node, independent of which
   resource tier it occupies. Stronger nodes do not absorb a disproportionate
   share of fees beyond what their additional links earn.
2. **Participation incentive** — Even capacity-bottlenecked `Micro`-tier
   nodes obtain non-negative profit in the majority of runs, so the
   long-run health of the network is preserved against the natural pressure
   toward centralisation.
3. **Attack-resistance under heterogeneity (G3)** — The Sybil attack remains
   unprofitable for adversaries of *every* tier, including those with
   substantially higher physical resources than the average honest node, so
   the security argument of §7.2 does not rely on uniform cloud nodes.

We omit a separate active-user-node experiment under heterogeneity for
space; we verified that the qualitative result of Fig. 4 carries over —
the profit rate stays negative across all four adversary tiers and all
`(w₀, fee)` settings of §8.3.
